About MA Bandit


Some have said that the market is impossible to predict: that no matter what tools you develop you just can’t beat it. The way most people treat the stock market is nothing short of gambling. We don’t gamble.

Our approach is derived from the standard model of particle physics which is an incredibly accurate coupling equation that describes the movement of particles. The standard model is an incredibly impressive feat of science, and it gives a lot of hope for systematizing complex structures—like prices in the stock market.

Now take a step back and ask yourself what moves market prices. Tax policies? War? Crop yields in Guam? It’s us who move the market— people. What we have developed, and continue to refine are the elements and their interactions in our standard model.

We are but a humble group of math, physics and tech nerds motivated by our success in modeling seemingly chaotic systems. Our team draws on an eclectic mix of disciplines to create new approaches to tame complexity.

Research


There are a lot of stocks you could invest in. As an investor you could evenly distribute your money across every stock and derivative and probably net a couple of percentage points of profit.

That is great, but those of you familiar with the 80-20 rule know that you want to be putting your real money in those stocks which are going to give you the big returns. This is the classic multi-armed bandit problem.

We hope to use group/game theory to clean our data, such that we transform the multi-armed bandit problem of the stock market to one of several solved cases.

Group theory is very important to the standard model; in fact the model has been shown to be isomorphic to the unitary product group SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1). Each of these groups contain  elements representing lagrangians, which in turn represent how a particle will move in a given field. These elements are sometimes referred to as “coupling constants”, and using the principle of least resistance describe both newtonian and relativistic motion to an astounding degree of accuracy.

The key to discovering coupling constants experimentally is to take lots and lots of unbiased data. Removing bias in experiments concerning humans is not an easy thing to do, and has been an issue endemic to data mining in finance. Naturalistic observation requires data to be taken without removing the participants from their daily environments, and a truly unbiased experiment on the behaviors of humans should be in some form a version of natural observation.

Our analytics then jumps into work evaluating how clean the data is. If we have the right type of information such that we can use an approximate solving strategy like contextual-epsilon-greedy or bayesian bandit to give us a prediction, then we put in some cash and see how we do. The feedback is clear, and our incentives as researchers are 100% in the right place with this model of testing.
